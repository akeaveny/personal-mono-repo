# Golf — Course Management & Mindset

## Mindset

### Process Over Outcome
- Focus on executing each shot with a clear plan, not on the result.
- A good shot with a bad outcome is still a good decision. A bad shot with a good outcome is still a bad decision.
- Judge yourself on commitment and process, not where the ball ends up.
- Pre-shot routine is the process — same every time, regardless of situation.

### Focus on the Positives
- After every round, identify 3-5 shots you executed well. Write them down.
- Negative self-talk compounds. One bad hole doesn't define the round.
- Reset after every shot. The next shot is the only one that matters.
- Confidence is built by remembering good shots, not by dwelling on bad ones.

## Strategy

### Build Your Score
- Par is your friend. Bogey is not the enemy — double bogey is.
- Eliminate the big numbers. A round with all bogeys and a few pars beats a round with birdies and triples.
- Think in terms of "what's the worst that can happen from here?" and avoid that.
- Play to your strengths, not to the course's challenges.

### Aggressive Off the Tee
- The tee shot is the one shot where you dictate terms — take advantage.
- Hit driver when the reward outweighs the risk. Shorter approach > safer tee shot in most cases.
- Commit fully. A committed 3-wood is better than a tentative driver, but a committed driver is best.
- Know your miss pattern and aim away from it.

### Conservative Into Greens
- Aim for the fat of the green, not the pin.
- Miss on the side that leaves an easy up-and-down (short-side misses are score killers).
- A 30-foot putt from the middle of the green is better than a chip from a tough lie.
- When the pin is tucked, play to the center — the pin will come to you over 18 holes.

## Know Your Game

### Understand Your Yardages
- Every club has a **carry number** — know it, trust it, use it.
- Carry is what matters, not total distance. Carry clears hazards, total doesn't help if you're in the water.
- Build a yardage card and update it periodically (range sessions or on-course tracking).
- Account for conditions: elevation, wind, temperature, lie — but start from accurate carry numbers.

### Read Your Game on the Day
- Warm-up tells you what shot shape you have today — don't fight it.
- If you're drawing it, play for the draw. If you're fading it, play for the fade.
- Adjust your strategy based on what's working, not what you wish was working.
- Some days you have your A-game, some days you don't. Managing your B-game is where scoring happens.

## Shot Selection

### Only Shots That Lead to a Scoring Difference
- Before every shot, ask: "Does the hero shot actually save me a stroke vs. the safe play?"
- Most of the time, the answer is no. The safe play and the aggressive play end up within half a stroke.
- The shots that matter most for scoring: tee shots, putts inside 10 feet, and chips/pitches within 50 yards.
- Don't waste mental energy on shots that don't move the needle — execute simply and move on.

## Course Management Playbook

The goal is simple: get the ball in the hole in as few strokes as possible. That means playing the highest-percentage shot every time, not the most exciting one.

### Thinking Backwards from the Green
- Start every hole from the green and work backwards. Where do I want to putt from? Where do I need to land my approach? Where does that mean I need to be off the tee?
- The tee shot isn't about distance — it's about setting up the best angle and yardage into the green.
- On par 5s: where do I want my third shot from? That dictates the layup, which dictates the tee shot.

### Hole-by-Hole Decision Framework
Before every shot, run through:
1. **What's my target?** — Pick a specific spot, not a general area.
2. **What club gets me there?** — Use carry number, not "feel."
3. **Where's the trouble?** — Water, OB, bunkers, short-sided pins.
4. **Where's the bail-out?** — Every shot needs an acceptable miss. If there's no bail-out, you're being too aggressive.
5. **Commit and execute.** — Once the decision is made, trust it fully.

### Par 3 Management
- These are the easiest holes to make par on — treat them that way.
- Club selection is everything. Take the club that carries to the **middle** of the green.
- If the pin is front, don't try to land it pin-high — you're flirting with short. Middle of the green, two-putt, move on.
- If the pin is back, one extra club to the center still leaves a reasonable putt.

### Par 4 Management
- Divide par 4s into two categories: **driver holes** and **positioning holes**.
- Driver holes: wide fairway, no real trouble, shorter approach = hit driver.
- Positioning holes: tight, trouble on one side, specific yardage needed = hit whatever leaves your favorite approach distance.
- Know your favorite approach yardage (e.g., full PW at 130) and try to leave yourself that number.

### Par 5 Management
- Par 5s are birdie opportunities but also where big numbers hide.
- Unless you can reach the green in two with a high-percentage shot, lay up to your best wedge distance.
- A 80-yard wedge into the green is far more valuable than a 220-yard hero shot that ends up in a bunker.
- Think of par 5s as three-shot holes. Three good shots = birdie putt. That's the plan.

### Playing from Trouble
- When you're in trouble, the **only** goal is to get back in play. Not to advance it as far as possible — to get back in play.
- Punch out sideways. Take your medicine. A bogey from trouble is a win; a double is the expected outcome of trying to be a hero.
- If you're in the trees, your target is the fairway. Period.
- If you're in a bad lie, take more club and swing easy — don't try to muscle it.

### Wind Management
- Into wind: take 1-2 extra clubs, swing smooth. Swinging harder adds spin, which makes the ball balloon.
- Downwind: the ball will release more on landing. Club down and let the wind do the work.
- Crosswind: aim into it and let it bring the ball back. Don't try to fight it with shot shape.
- Wind affects short game too — factor it into chips and pitches, not just full shots.

## Scoring Zones

Where strokes are actually gained and lost. Practice time should reflect these priorities.

### 100 Yards and In (Where Scoring Happens)
- This is where the round is made. Approach shots, chips, pitches, bunker shots.
- Know your **wedge distances cold**: full, 3/4, and 1/2 swings for each wedge.
- Distance control matters more than direction here. A shot that's the right distance but 15 feet offline is fine. A shot that's perfect line but 20 yards short is a disaster.

### Putting
- Speed is more important than line. Almost always.
- On long putts (30+ feet), focus on getting the ball within a 3-foot circle. Two-putts are the goal.
- On mid-range putts (10-30 feet), read the break, commit to the line, control speed.
- Inside 10 feet is where you make or break a round. These are the putts worth practicing relentlessly.
- Never leave a birdie putt short. Aggressive line, die it in the front edge at worst.

### Up-and-Down (Scrambling)
- When you miss a green, the goal is simple: get up and down.
- Pick the shot with the most green to work with. Putt from off the green if possible. Chip if you can't putt. Pitch only when you have to.
- The less air time, the more predictable the shot. Keep it low and let it run.
- Don't get cute. A boring bump-and-run to 8 feet beats a fancy flop that could go anywhere.

## Post-Round Review

After every round, answer these honestly:
1. **Where did I drop strokes?** — Tee shots? Approaches? Short game? Putting?
2. **How many penalty strokes?** — These are the most avoidable strokes in golf.
3. **How many 3-putts?** — Usually a speed control issue, not a reading issue.
4. **Did I have a plan on every shot?** — Or did I just step up and swing?
5. **What's one thing to work on before the next round?**

Track these over time. Patterns will emerge, and patterns tell you where to practice.
