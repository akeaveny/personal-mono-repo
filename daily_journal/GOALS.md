# Goals

## Health: Stop optimizing for how I look, start optimizing for how I feel

### The shift

Move away from gym bro culture — heavy lifting, high protein obsession, calorie counting — and toward a life built around energy, longevity, and how I actually feel day to day. Biking, running, eating real food, steady energy.

### Problem: Binge eating

This is the first thing to address. Everything else (training changes, meal plan shifts) is secondary until this pattern is under control.

**The pattern:**
- Binges happen on **unstructured evenings alone**, especially Thursday/Friday when nothing is planned
- Also triggered by **drinking** or being outside normal routine
- The binge fills a void — when structure disappears, food becomes the activity
- The "clean vs bad food" framing makes it worse: a single "bad" choice feels like failure, which triggers the full binge ("already ruined it")

**Root cause:** It's not about food. It's about what I do with unstructured time alone. The binge is the symptom.

### Problem: Unstructured weekends and the productivity trap

Same root cause as the binge cycle, different symptom. Weekends without a plan create anxiety about not being productive — but there's also a resistance to "always working." The result is paralysis: not working, not resting, not doing anything intentional. Then a slip-up (binge, wasted day) compounds the guilt and bleeds into Monday.

**The cycle:**
1. Weekend arrives with no plan
2. Anxiety kicks in — "I should be doing something productive"
3. But also don't want to feel like I'm always working
4. End up doing nothing intentional, which feels worse than either option
5. Slip-up happens, guilt spirals, productivity drops further

**Root cause:** No distinction between "productive," "intentional rest," and "wasted time." Right now only *working* counts as a good use of time, which makes everything else feel like failure. A bike ride, cooking a meal, or watching a movie needs to count as a win — if it was planned and intentional.

### Action plan: Phase 1 — Structure the danger zones

These rules address both problems (binge eating and wasted weekends) because they share the same root cause.

**Rule 1: Plan Thu/Fri evenings and weekends by Wednesday.**
Not every minute — just have *something* for each block. It can be productive, social, or purely fun. The only bad option is *no plan*. Put it on the calendar so it's real.

**Rule 2: Redefine "productive."**
A weekend is successful if the time was **intentional**. A long bike ride, cooking a new recipe, watching a movie you picked on purpose — these are wins. The only failure mode is drifting into nothing because you couldn't decide between working and resting.

**Rule 3: When the "mood" hits, name it.**
Before acting on it, write one sentence in the journal: "The mood is here. I'm feeling ___." This isn't about stopping the binge through willpower — it's about building awareness of the trigger so it loses automatic power over time.

**Rule 4: Eat enough during the day.**
Most binges start because you underate earlier. If you're restricting during the day to "save" calories, you're setting up the binge. Eat real meals. Full portions. No calorie math.

**Rule 5: Drop the "clean vs bad" labels.**
There is no "clean" and "bad." There's food. Some of it is more nutritious, some is more fun. Eating pizza on a Thursday doesn't mean you failed. The binge happens when one choice becomes "I already messed up, might as well go all in."

### Weekly planning ritual

Every **Wednesday evening**, spend 10 minutes filling in:
- **Thursday evening:** ___
- **Friday evening:** ___
- **Saturday:** ___ (morning block) / ___ (afternoon block)
- **Sunday:** ___ (morning block) / ___ (afternoon block)

Each slot gets one of: social plans, a workout/ride/run, a project, errands, or intentional rest (named — "watch X," "read," "cook Y"). If a slot is blank by Thursday morning, that's the red flag.

### Tracking

Use `/journal` to log:
- When the mood hits — not to track food, but to track the *feeling*
- Weekend planning on Wednesday — what you planned vs what you actually did
- Over time this builds a map of triggers, what helped, and what didn't

### What success looks like

- Thu/Fri/weekends have a plan before they arrive
- "Rest" and "fun" feel like legitimate uses of time, not failure
- Fewer unplanned, empty evenings
- When a binge does happen, it's smaller and I recover faster (no "week is ruined" spiral)
- Eventually: eating is boring and automatic, not an emotional event
