# Citrini Research — Strait of Hormuz: A Field Trip

## Summary

An extraordinary first-person field report from Citrini Research. Their analyst ("Analyst #3") physically traveled to the Strait of Hormuz via Dubai, Fujairah, and Oman's Musandam peninsula during the active Gulf War III conflict in early April 2026. He spoke with ship brokers, commodity traders, Omani officials, Iranian smugglers, fishermen in Kumzar, and a Greek vessel captain — then took a speedboat into the Strait itself, 18 miles from the Iranian coast, with Shahed drones and AIS-off tankers visible. He was detained twice (Omani CID and Coast Guard). The core finding: the market's binary framing ("Strait open or closed") is wrong — it's operating as an Iranian-managed toll road.

## Key Claims

1. **The Strait is NOT closed — it's an "Iranian toll road."** Ships are transiting with IRGC approval via an "Iranian Toll Booth" process. A Greek vessel captain described queuing to receive Iranian approval. Ships that comply pass through; those that refuse are the ones getting hit. The market has been pricing a blockade, but the reality on the water is a toll road.

2. **AIS data significantly understates actual traffic.** Fishermen in Kumzar reported 4-5 tankers passing daily completely dark on AIS (no tracking). Citrini's team confirmed 15 ships crossing on April 2nd using visual count from shore + Chinese phone signal. Contacts reported 15-18 more on April 4th — that's the prior week's worth of traffic in two days. Traffic is accelerating through the Qeshm channel.

3. **The conflict is more violent than reported but also more "normal" than expected.** Drone strikes on civilian vessels and fishing boats are happening regularly and unreported. But simultaneously, commerce continues — Iranian smugglers cross daily running contraband (electronics, cigarettes, alcohol) with no decrease in illicit traffic. "Both more ships and more strikes" simultaneously.

4. **Fujairah port operations at ~30% of pre-conflict levels.** Workers at GPS Chemical and Chem Oil confirmed operations severely degraded but port is back to operational. Ruwais got hit worse. Tank farm damage visible.

5. **Iran is using the situation as a sovereignty assertion.** Multiple Omani sources — officials, Kumzari fishermen, and the Greek captain — all described the same dynamic: Iran is asserting that it controls passage, can enforce safety under its management, and commerce will flow regardless of what the US does. It's propaganda + practical management combined.

6. **The ceasefire is fragile and local sources expect re-escalation.** Dubai poker table sources (commodity traders, Gulf businesspeople) predicted strikes on Qeshm — which happened four days later. American soldiers were amassing at higher rates than reported. The ceasefire framing as "peace" is premature.

## Thesis Impact

- **Impact:** MIXED
- **Tickers affected:** CVX, OXY, LNG, VLO, DVN
- **Claims touched:**
  - *MIXED* — Energy supply disruption trigger. The thesis says "Strait of Hormuz closed" — but Citrini's ground truth says it's not closed, it's a toll road. This is bearish for the most extreme energy price scenarios (full blockade) but bullish for sustained elevated prices (toll road = friction premium, not supply cut). The $110→$96→$102 Brent trajectory makes more sense with this framing.
  - *SUPPORTS* — Geopolitical risk remains extreme. The ceasefire is fragile, drone strikes continue on civilian targets, and local intelligence expects re-escalation. This supports maintaining energy overweight.
  - *CONTRADICTS* — "Strait of Hormuz reopening" as a take-profits trigger. The Citrini report suggests the Strait never fully closed — it shifted to managed passage. So a "reopening" is more of a spectrum than a binary event. The take-profits signal needs to be reframed.

## What I'm Taking Away

- The binary "open/closed" framing in the thesis is wrong. The Strait is operating on a spectrum of Iranian control. This means the energy premium won't collapse to zero on a "reopening" — there's a permanent friction premium as long as Iran maintains its toll-road posture. Bullish for sustained elevated energy prices, bearish for another spike.
- AIS data is unreliable — 50%+ of traffic is dark. Any analysis (including Cembalest's Pandora's Bog) that relies on AIS vessel counts is underestimating actual flow. This is important context.
- The ceasefire collapsing is the key near-term signal. Local intelligence is more bearish on the ceasefire holding than the market is pricing. Energy names should be held, not trimmed on ceasefire optimism.
- This is the best piece of primary-source intelligence I've read on the Gulf situation. The ground-truth framing of "toll road, not blockade" should be incorporated into the thesis.
