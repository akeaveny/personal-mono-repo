# Pandora's Bog: The Global Energy Shock of 2026

## Summary

Michael Cembalest's 13-page J.P. Morgan "Eye on the Market" energy paper from March 21, 2026, analyzes what he calls "Gulf War III" — the largest oil supply disruption since WWII. The Strait of Hormuz is mostly closed, with ~20% of global oil markets disrupted (14-15 mbd crude + 5 mbd petrochemical feedstocks stranded) and zero spare capacity globally. Qatar LNG has been physically damaged (2 of 14 trains, 12.8 MM tonnes lost for 3-5 years). Oil prices are soaring, petrochemical prices up 30-100%+, fertilizer prices surging. The US is acting unilaterally without NATO allies. The IEA SPR release was ignored by markets. Iran still effectively controls the Strait.

## Key Claims

1. **Largest oil supply disruption since WWII.** 20% of global oil supply disrupted with 0% spare capacity — dwarfs every prior crisis including the 1973 Arab embargo (7% disrupted, 8% spare), 1990 Gulf War (9%, 4%), and 2003 Gulf War II (7%, 1%). This is unprecedented in the modern era.

2. **Strait of Hormuz is mostly closed.** Out of ~21 mbd crude + products via Hormuz, only 6-7 mbd has found alternative routes (Red Sea, Fujairah). 14-15 mbd still stranded. If closed for another 3-4 weeks, physical shortages will curtail economic activity starting in Asia.

3. **Qatar LNG infrastructure physically damaged.** 2 of 14 LNG trains and one gas-to-liquids facility hit. 12.8 MM tonnes of LNG lost for 3-5 years (17% of Qatari output). Force Majeure declared on contracts to Italy, Belgium, Korea, China. Qatar's planned ~30 MT/yr expansion delayed/halted.

4. **Cascading commodity shock across the entire spectrum.** Jet fuel up 100%+ (Singapore, NW Europe), wholesale gasoline up 60-100%+ (Japan, India), petrochemicals (propylene +53-78%, ethylene +42-63%), fertilizers surging (urea, ammonia — Gulf is 43% of global urea, 44% of sulfur, 27% of ammonia). Methanol up ~50% (Gulf = 40% of global supply). Helium spot +100% (Qatar = 34% of global supply).

5. **US insulated on gas but not on oil.** Henry Hub barely moved — US domestic gas production is the shield. But crude oil is a global market; despite being a net exporter, US gasoline/diesel prices rising. US equity market -4% since conflict start. If $120 oil sustained 6 months, S&P 500 EPS falls ~$12.7 (4%).

6. **China is surprisingly insulated.** Heavy coal reliance + domestic gas production = 76% "total insulation factor." Most exposed: Italy, Taiwan, Japan, Korea, Singapore, Spain, Netherlands, Belgium. Japan at 56% oil+imported gas share of primary energy, Italy similar.

7. **US acting alone, without NATO coalition.** After tariffing and mocking NATO allies for a year, they are not predisposed to help reopen Hormuz. Iran controls safe passage and demands include per-ship fees and removal of US bases from the Gulf.

## What I'm Taking Away

- **The thesis trigger has fired.** The "Energy supply disruption" signal from the "What I'm Watching" table said: "Add to energy positions." This is not a drill — this is the largest disruption since WWII.
- **CVX, OXY, DVN are the primary beneficiaries** — US-based production with domestic operations. OXY especially as Berkshire's 28% holding and upstream-focused (most leveraged to oil price).
- **VLO (refining) should outperform** — tight refined product markets with crack spreads blowing out (jet fuel, gasoline, diesel all surging faster than crude).
- **LNG/Cheniere gets a reprieve** — the supply glut thesis is dead for now. Qatar damage (3-5 years) means US LNG exports are critical. This completely changes the Cheniere outlook.
- **CCJ/nuclear gets another tailwind** — countries will accelerate nuclear buildout to reduce dependence on geopolitically risky fossil fuel imports. Long-term uranium demand reinforced.
- **The $120+ oil / 4% S&P EPS hit scenario** — validates the defensive posture for core portfolio. BND and defensive names provide cushion.
- **This crisis will reshape energy policy globally** — just like the 1970s, expect a global push for energy efficiency and reduced reliance on geopolitically risky imports.
