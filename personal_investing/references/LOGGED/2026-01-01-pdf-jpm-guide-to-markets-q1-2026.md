# J.P. Morgan Guide to the Markets — US Q1 2026

## Summary

J.P. Morgan's iconic 71-page quarterly market reference covering equity valuations, earnings, fixed income, macro, international markets, and asset allocation. As of Dec 31, 2025: S&P 500 at 22.0x forward P/E (30-year avg 17.1x), top 10 companies at 41.1% of index market cap, energy at only 2.8% index weight, IG credit spreads at 2nd percentile (78bps), consumer sentiment at 52.9, fed funds expected to decline to 3.1%, and federal debt at 99.7% of GDP heading to 115.5%.

## Key Claims

1. **S&P 500 valuations are stretched.** Forward P/E of 22.0x vs 30-year average of 17.1x (+1 std dev is 20.4x). Shiller CAPE at 39.8x vs 28.5x avg. At this valuation, R² = 30% for subsequent 5-year returns, with the regression implying muted forward returns. Earnings yield spread vs Baa is -0.5% (vs 0.7% avg) — stocks aren't compensating for risk vs bonds.

2. **Market concentration is extreme.** Top 10 companies = 41.1% of S&P 500 market cap and 32.6% of earnings. Mag 7 returned +23% in 2025 vs +13% for S&P 493. Mag 7 at 29.0x forward P/E vs 19.3x for remaining companies. Concentration has not been this high since the dot-com peak.

3. **Energy is profoundly underrepresented.** Only 2.8% of S&P 500 weight. Forward P/E of 15.8x (vs 20-year avg 13.7x), but dividend yield of 3.5% (vs 2.9% avg). Value stocks broadly at -40% P/E discount to growth (vs -30% long-term avg) — value hasn't been this cheap relative to growth in a long time.

4. **Credit markets are complacent.** IG spreads at 78bps (2nd percentile), HY at 266bps (2nd percentile). Extremely tight spreads leave no buffer for deterioration. Fixed income yield-to-worst across sectors ranges from 3.6% (munis) to 8.1% (leveraged loans).

5. **Consumer sentiment is depressed but balance sheets are healthy.** University of Michigan at 52.9 (avg 77.5). Historically, trough readings have preceded +24.1% average 12-month S&P returns. Household debt service ratio at 11.6% — well below the 15.8% 2007 peak. Top 20% holds 51% of income, drives spending.

6. **Federal deficit and debt trajectory is alarming.** $1.775T deficit, debt/GDP at 99.7% heading to 115.5% by 2034 (CBO). Net interest payments now $970B (14% of federal spending). OBBBA and tariffs make projections worse.

7. **International equities at deep discounts.** ACWI ex-US at 14.9x forward P/E vs US at 22.0x. Eurozone at 12.3x, EM at 12.0x, China at 10.1x. The gap is historically wide.

## Thesis Impact

- **Impact:** SUPPORTS
- **Tickers affected:** Broad market
- **Claims touched:**
  - *STRONGLY SUPPORTS* — "The risk/reward for broad equities has deteriorated" — S&P 500 at 22x (30yr avg 17.1x), concentration extreme (41.1% top 10), earnings yield spread negative. This is the data backing the defensive posture.
  - *SUPPORTS* — "Energy stocks offer real cash flows and dividends" — energy at only 2.8% of S&P with 3.5% dividend yield and 15.8x P/E, confirming it's underowned and undervalued relative to the index
  - *SUPPORTS* — "Credit conditions tightening" — spreads at 2nd percentile, the only direction from here is wider. Credit market complacency aligns with recession risk being underpriced.
  - *SUPPORTS* — "Diversification away from US concentration risk" — international ex-US at 14.9x vs 22.0x US. The data strongly argues for VXUS allocation in core.

## What I'm Taking Away

- This is the single best visual confirmation of the defensive thesis. Valuations, concentration, credit complacency, and fiscal trajectory all scream caution. The 22.0x forward P/E with -0.5% earnings yield spread is the clearest signal that broad equity expected returns are low.
- Energy at 2.8% S&P weight is striking — if there's any mean reversion or supply disruption, the sector is massively underweight in passive flows. This supports the satellite energy overweight.
- Credit spreads at 2nd percentile is a ticking time bomb. When they widen, it will correlate with equity drawdowns. BND allocation provides some hedge but monitor closely.
- International discount (10-15x vs 22x US) supports maintaining VXUS in core — reversion has historically occurred but timing is uncertain.
- Consumer sentiment at 52.9 is actually contrarian bullish for 12-month returns (+24.1% avg from troughs) — be ready for the market to rally despite the bad vibes.
